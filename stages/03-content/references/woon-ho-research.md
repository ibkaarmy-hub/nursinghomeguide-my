## A. Pricing

The only published figures come from the facility's own "Monthly Cost for Nursing Home in Johor Bahru" page.

- Monthly cost band: "starting from RM2,000 and above"; can "go as high as RM6,000 per month".
- No tier breakdown is published — i.e. no rate card showing what RM2,000 covers vs what triggers RM6,000.
- No itemisation of what is or is not included. The website does not state whether the following are included or charged separately:
  - Diapers
  - Medication / pharmacy
  - Doctor consultations beyond the two complimentary monthly visits
  - Milk feeds / nutritional supplements
  - Oxygen
  - Wound dressings / consumables
  - Physiotherapy sessions
  - Transport / ambulance use
  - Laundry
- No published deposit, booking fee, daily rate, minimum stay, payment frequency, or refund policy.

**Social-worker note:** The pricing band is consistent with the JB market range, but the absence of a tier breakdown and an inclusions list means a family cannot estimate true monthly outlay without a phone call. Expect the RM6,000 ceiling to apply to higher-dependency residents (tube-fed, bedridden, dementia with behavioural needs); confirm in writing before admission.

Specific clinical conditions or care types named:

- Parkinson's care

- Disability support
- Wound care ("wound dressing services… to clean, cover and protect various type of the wounds")
- Tube feeding ("patients that have difficulty of eating and required tube feeding") — NG vs PEG is not specified
- Catheter / urinary care ("management of urine catheter")
- Psychosocial support
- Medication management
- General elderly / retirement residency

**Not explicitly addressed** anywhere on the site: oxygen-dependent residents, ventilated residents, dialysis-dependent residents (transport arrangements), bariatric residents, infectious-disease isolation, or admission of residents with active behavioural / wandering risk in dementia.

**Social-worker note:** The list is broad and includes palliative and tube-feeding language, but is written in marketing prose, not clinical-criteria language. A family with a high-acuity referral (e.g. PEG + tracheostomy, oxygen >2 L/min, advanced dementia with wandering) should ask for a written admission assessment and confirm capability before signing.

## C. Staffing

Claims drawn from the website, in the language used:

- "24-Hour Nursing Care: Highly trained nurses and caregivers are always available."
- "Experienced nurses and caregivers' team."
- "Free doctor consultations are provided twice a month" / "Complimentary doctor consultation, twice a month."
- On-call doctors referenced.
- Physiotherapy: "Tailored programs help residents regain strength" / "advanced physiotherapy and rehabilitation programme."
- Staff "trained in elderly care and nursing care."

**Not stated:**

- Whether a Registered Nurse (RN) is physically on-site 24/7, or whether overnight cover is by caregivers with RN on call.
- Caregiver-to-resident ratios for day or night shifts.
- Number of RNs, caregivers, or total headcount.
- Whether the physiotherapist is in-house full-time, part-time, or visiting; session frequency per resident.
- Whether occupational therapy or speech therapy is offered.
- Languages spoken by staff (Chinese-language version of the site exists, suggesting Mandarin/Chinese-dialect capability; English content is present; Bahasa Malaysia capability not stated).

**Social-worker note:** "24-hour nursing care" is a marketing phrase that in Malaysian SME care homes commonly means caregivers on duty round the clock, with RN cover during day shifts only. Two complimentary doctor visits per month is below the frequency a high-acuity resident typically needs — extra visits will likely be billed. Ask explicitly: "Is an RN physically present overnight, or only on call?" and "What is the day and night caregiver-to-resident ratio?"

- AGECOPE member: yes, listed in the AGECOPE national directory (Association for Residential Aged Care Operators of Malaysia).
- JKM (Jabatan Kebajikan Masyarakat) registration number: **not published on the website**.

**Social-worker note:** JKM registration is the single most important regulatory check for a Malaysian care home. The absence of a published JKM number does not mean the home is unregistered — many homes simply don't display it — but families should ask to see the JKM certificate on visit. AGECOPE membership is a positive industry signal but is not a regulator.

## H. Red Flags and Strong Points

**Strong points**

- AGECOPE member, suggesting some industry-level engagement and willingness to be listed publicly.
- Concrete fall-prevention infrastructure named (grab bars, non-slip floors, bed alarms).
- 24-hour CCTV explicitly stated.
- Outdoor garden / large compound is a real differentiator vs shophouse-style JB homes.
- Free / "complimentary" twice-monthly doctor visits — a structured baseline most ad-hoc homes lack.
- Range of services covers stroke, dementia, palliative, tube feeding and wound care — appropriate scope for a mid-acuity resident.
- Bilingual web presence (English + Chinese) with named founders, suggesting owner-operator transparency.

**Red flags / gaps to probe**

- No published JKM registration number.
- No room types, bed counts, or A/C details published.
- No itemised pricing — RM2k–RM6k band is too wide to plan a budget.
- No published caregiver-to-resident ratio for day or night.
- No clarity on whether an RN is physically on-site overnight.
- Halal status not declared (matters for Muslim families).
- Visiting hours, deposit, refund policy, minimum stay all unpublished.
- "Palliative care" is named but not detailed — ask about syringe driver, DNR, and end-of-life pathway.
- No dialysis-transport, oxygen, or tracheostomy detail — likely not high-acuity-equipped.
